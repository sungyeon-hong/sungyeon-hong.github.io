Musical Homeostasis v.2: Distributed Tuning

A simplified online demo of self-organisation through music. Each triangle is an adaptive musical agent that plays one note, listens only to neighbouring triangles, and may retune itself to improve local harmony. The whole lattice becomes a collective instrument.

What this models

This demo explores distributed tuning: no cell sees the whole system, and there is no central conductor. Yet a coherent collective sound may emerge from local interaction alone.

Visual pattern and heard chord are directly linked: more triangles of a given colour means that note is heard more strongly.

Rules

  1. Each triangle holds one current note.
  2. At each step, it listens to its three edge-neighbours.
  3. It may switch to the note that best fits its local musical neighbourhood.
  4. A little noise keeps the system flexible, and disturbances test recovery.

What to look for

  • Start with Initialise, then Run.
  • Listen for whether the sound settles into near-unison or a chord.
  • Watch whether coloured domains form, move, or dissolve.
  • Use Disturb patch to see whether coherence returns.

Remark: this is inspired by cellular automata such as Conway's Game of Life, but instead of birth and death, the cells remain present and retune. That is why we call it distributed tuning.

Pitch distribution

These bars drive the sound directly. If one note dominates, you will hear near-unison. If several compatible notes persist, you will hear a chord.

Questions to explore

  • How much tuning strength is needed for a coherent musical field to emerge?
  • Does a disturbance fade away or reorganise the whole lattice?
  • What happens when you increase the note set from a triad to the chromatic scale?
  • Is equilibrium more interesting as sameness, or as compatible difference?
Homeostasis here is not static silence. It is the tendency of the musical field to recover or maintain coherence while continually adjusting through local feedback.
